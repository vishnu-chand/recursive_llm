### **Part IX: Board-Style Review Questions**

**Q1. The "Stone Heart" Theory**
A 74-year-old female presents with confusion, vomiting, and yellow vision. Her ECG shows atrial fibrillation with slow ventricular response and "scooped" ST depressions. Labs reveal K+ 6.8 mEq/L and Digoxin level 3.9 ng/mL. Which of the following is the **MOST** appropriate initial step for managing her hyperkalemia?

A. IV Calcium Gluconate 1g
B. IV Insulin 10 units + Dextrose 50%
C. Oral Sodium Polystyrene Sulfonate (Kayexalate)
D. Emergent Hemodialysis
E. IV Amiodarone

> **Answer:** **B. IV Insulin 10 units + Dextrose 50%**
> * **Explanation:** While Calcium is the standard first-line treatment for hyperkalemia to stabilize the cardiac membrane, it is classically contraindicated (or used with extreme caution) in **Digoxin Toxicity**. The "Stone Heart" theory suggests calcium can precipitate irreversible myocardial contraction in this setting. Therefore, shifting potassium (Insulin/Glucose) and binding Digoxin (DigiFab) takes priority.
> * *Why not C?* Kayexalate is too slow.
> * *Why not D?* Dialysis is invasive and she is hemodynamically unstable.
> * *Why not E?* Amiodarone increases Digoxin levels and worsens the toxicity.

**Q2. The Drug Interaction Cascade**
The patient's INR was found to be 7.5. She was taking Warfarin and Amiodarone. Which mechanism best describes why Amiodarone contributed to the supratherapeutic INR?

A. Amiodarone displaces Warfarin from plasma proteins.
B. Amiodarone inhibits CYP2C9, reducing the clearance of Warfarin.
C. Amiodarone induces CYP3A4, increasing Warfarin metabolism.
D. Amiodarone causes direct hepatotoxicity, reducing clotting factor synthesis.

> **Answer:** **B. Amiodarone inhibits CYP2C9, reducing the clearance of Warfarin.**
> * **Explanation:** Amiodarone is a potent inhibitor of CYP2C9 (and CYP1A2/CYP3A4). Warfarin is metabolized by these enzymes. When added, Amiodarone can increase Warfarin concentrations by 30-50% or more. The dose of Warfarin usually needs to be reduced by half when starting Amiodarone.

**Q3. The Synovial Analysis**
Ortho performs an arthrocentesis on the patient's prosthetic knee. Which of the following synovial fluid results is **MOST** specific for a diagnosis of Septic Arthritis in a prosthetic joint?

A. WBC > 1,100 /µL with > 64% Neutrophils.
B. WBC > 50,000 /µL with > 90% Neutrophils.
C. Glucose levels equal to serum glucose.
D. Presence of monosodium urate crystals.

> **Answer:** **A. WBC > 1,100 /µL with > 64% Neutrophils.**
> * **Explanation:** This is a "Tricky" nuance!
> * In a **Native Knee**, Septic Arthritis is usually defined as WBC > 50,000.
> * In a **Prosthetic Knee**, the threshold is *much lower* (WBC > 1,100 to 3,000) because the joint capsule is altered and biofilms form. If you waited for 50,000 in a prosthetic knee, you would miss the diagnosis.
> * *Note:* The presence of crystals (D) would indicate Gout, but Gout and Septic Arthritis can coexist!

**Q4. The Adrenal Mystery**
The patient had hypotension resistant to fluids and a history of chronic steroid use. Which electrolyte abnormality is classically associated with **Primary** Adrenal Insufficiency (Addison's) but **NOT** usually seen in Secondary Adrenal Insufficiency (e.g., from stopping Prednisone)?

A. Hyponatremia
B. Hyperkalemia
C. Hypercalcemia
D. Hypoglycemia

> **Answer:** **B. Hyperkalemia**
> * **Explanation:**
> * **Primary AI (Addison's):** The adrenal gland itself is destroyed. You lose Cortisol AND **Aldosterone**. Loss of Aldosterone causes salt wasting (Hyponatremia) and potassium retention (**Hyperkalemia**).
> * **Secondary AI (Steroid withdrawal):** The problem is the Pituitary (ACTH). ACTH controls Cortisol, but the RAAS system controls Aldosterone. Therefore, Aldosterone is usually intact, and K+ remains normal.
> * *The Trap in this Case:* Mrs. Vance had Hyperkalemia *despite* this likely being Secondary AI. Why? Because of the **Renal Failure** and **Spironolactone**. (Medicine is never simple!)
